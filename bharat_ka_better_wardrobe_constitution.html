01
Foundation
The Idea

One paragraph. This is what every writer, director, creator, and new joiner reads first. It is the answer to "what is this campaign?"

Pre-filled draft — refine in session

Most Indians have spent their lives buying clothes designed for someone else — a different body, a different climate, a different life. सादा / SAADAA is building the wardrobe that should have always existed: a permanent, considered collection of basics made specifically for how India actually lives. Bharat Ka BETTER | बेहतर Wardrobe is the umbrella under which every product we launch lives. It is not a season. It is not a trend. It is a long-running declaration that Indians deserve better basics — and that we are here to build them, one fabric at a time.

Decision needed

Is the emotional register of this IP primarily warm and reassuring, or quietly confident and assertive? The draft above leans assertive. Align the team on the feeling before approving this paragraph — it will govern every creative instinct downstream.

Core Message
Pre-filled — locked

We all don't need more. Just better.

02
The Why
The Conviction

This section answers: why does this IP exist? It is the reasoning a founder would give on stage, a writer would reach for when they feel lost, and a creator would paraphrase naturally. It is not a tagline. It is a belief.

Pre-filled — three pillars of conviction

Designed for India, not borrowed from elsewhere. Every garment we make starts with the Indian body, the Indian summer, the Indian commute. Not as a positioning statement — as an actual design constraint. This is why we are transparent about the why behind every product we launch.

 

Permanence over newness. We are not building a collection for this season. We are building a wardrobe for the next decade. When something earns its place in the Behtar Wardrobe, it stays. We are not chasing fast fashion — we are building against it.

 

Democratising the decision to buy well. Knowing what is worth buying, what will last, what actually fits — this should not be a privilege. The Behtar Wardrobe thesis is that every Indian, regardless of city or income tier, deserves access to basics that are genuinely better.

Decision needed

The third pillar (democratisation) has pricing implications. Do you want to make a democratisation claim publicly in campaign materials, or keep it as internal conviction? If stated publicly, it will invite scrutiny of your price points. Decide the level of directness before this goes to creators.

03
Communication
The Voice

The voice of this IP is not a mood board. It is a set of specific, testable choices. When in doubt, run your copy through this section.

We say this
  • Breathes with you in July
  • Made for Indian skin tones
  • The fabric you'll reach for every day
  • Fits the way India actually moves
  • Soft enough to forget you're wearing it
  • Built to last five summers, not one
  • Behtar — not more
We never say this
  • Luxury, elevate, curate, premium
  • Moisture-wicking technology
  • Effortlessly chic
  • Less is more (too generic)
  • Wardrobe staple (overused)
  • Sustainable (unless we can prove it)
  • Fashion-forward or trend-led anything
Language Protocol
Pre-filled — locked

Verbal communication: Say Behtar — warm, conversational, Hindi-first.
Written / visual communication: Use BETTER | बेहतर — the bilingual mark, always together, always in this order.
Subtitles and text overlays: Minimal. When used, Helvetica only. Color: yellow on dark backgrounds, black or white on light.

Decision needed

Does the language switch by platform or stay consistent? For example: does a reel targeting a Tier 2 audience lead more heavily in Hindi, while a metro-targeted paid ad leads in English? Decide whether there is a platform-level language rule, or whether it is always creator/director discretion.

Decision needed

Can the campaign use humour? The current tone pillars (simple, honest, minimal, functional) don't say yes or no. Humour, used right, could be a strong tool — especially in prebuzz. Align the team: is there room for wit inside this IP, or does the IP always stay earnest?

05
Boundaries
What This Is Not

The most underrated section of any creative IP. Being explicit about what breaks the campaign is what keeps it coherent across 15 launches and multiple creators. If a piece of content matches any description below, it does not belong under this IP.

Pre-filled — do not cross

Not a fashion campaign. No editorial poses, no runway lighting, no clothes that look unwearable in real life. The moment a shot feels like a fashion magazine, it has broken the IP.

 

Not aspirational in the traditional sense. We are not showing the life people wish they had. We are showing the life people actually live, with better clothes in it. Aspirational settings (rooftops, cafes, airports) are fine only when they are real and unglamourised.

 

Not a "sustainability brand" campaign. We do not lead with sustainability as a message. If a creator or writer frames the campaign around eco-consciousness, it misrepresents the brand's actual positioning.

 

Not metro-only. If all the faces, all the settings, and all the references are from Mumbai and Bengaluru, the campaign has failed. Tier 2 and Tier 3 India must be visibly, genuinely present — not as a token shot at the end.

 

Not loud. No high-energy music drops, no fast cuts, no hype language. The IP's natural pace is calm and considered. Urgency is fine in prebuzz; it should never bleed into storytelling content.

Decision needed

Add 2–3 more "not" statements specific to what you've seen go wrong in past SAADAA content. The team will have examples. Surface them here so they are documented and not repeated.

06
Audience
The Persona Bank

These are the people this campaign speaks to. Each launch will draw on one or two personas as primary — never all four at once. For each persona, the most important line is the Wardrobe Truth — the one problem सादा / SAADAA solves for them. This is what writers and directors reach for when they need to make something feel real.

Decision needed — resolve before launch

The target audience header states 18–40 years. Two of the four personas below are 58 and 65. Either update the stated age range to reflect the actual audience, or clarify that these older personas represent aspirational future customers. This discrepancy will confuse media buyers and creators if left unresolved.

The School Teacher
42 · Tier 2 City · ₹15L income

Balances work and motherhood. Values comfort above all — approaching menopause, so breathability and soft fabrics matter more than they used to. Cooks, reads, socialises. Buys once and wears often.

Her Wardrobe Truth

She's tired of clothes that look good on the hanger but fail her by afternoon. She needs fabric that works as hard as she does.

The Software Engineer
58 · Tier 3 City · ₹60L income · Plus-size

Spends most of his time at work and with family. Manages diabetes — breathable fabrics are a health need, not a preference. Sticks to shirt-and-trouser silhouettes he knows. Comfort over trends, always.

His Wardrobe Truth

Most clothes aren't designed for his body or his life. When something actually fits and breathes, he buys three of it.

The Retired One
65 · Tier 2/3 City · Fixed routine

Simple, routine-driven life. A small, dependable wardrobe — not many pieces, but ones that can be trusted. Soft fabrics, loose fits, no fuss. Has long since stopped caring about trends.

Their Wardrobe Truth

They want clothes that disappear — that require no thought, no adjusting, no discomfort. Just reliable softness, every day.

The Marketing Executive
26 · Metro · ₹8–10L · Independent

Fast-paced, lives alone, values versatility. Needs outfits that go from desk to dinner without a change. Fuss-free and repeat-wear conscious. Aware of quality — will pay more for less, if it earns the repeat.

Her Wardrobe Truth

She's done buying things that look good once. She wants a uniform she's actually proud to repeat.

Decision needed

Do the Wardrobe Truths above match what your actual customer data says? You have 8 lakh+ customers and BigQuery. Before this goes to creators, validate at least 2 of these truths against return reason data, helpdesk themes, or review language. A truth grounded in real data will write better copy than one invented in a room.

Decision needed — resolve the door conflict

Prebuzz 1 uses a "knock on the door" hook. Launch 1 uses a "newspaper at the door" format. Both are door-based and meant to be repeatable. Running both in the same launch cycle risks making the campaign feel formulaic. Either merge them into one overarching door motif with intentional variation, or differentiate them more sharply so they feel distinct. Decide before the first shoot.

Decision needed — the brand film

The Concept Storytelling film (poetic, cinematic, brand film) is the most important piece in the entire IP — everything else is cut from it or references it. It currently has no brief of its own. Before any other content is shot, write a separate one-page brief for this film: what is the emotional arc, whose story does it follow, what is the first frame, what is the last feeling. This cannot be left to interpretation.

08
Creators
Influencer Philosophy
Pre-filled — core philosophy

The creator's story leads. Brand integration is natural and never transactional-feeling. A creator who genuinely wears and believes the product is worth more than one with 10x the followers who doesn't. We are building long-term associations, not one-time posts.

Pre-filled — non-negotiables for all creators

Must always include: Minimum 3 hooks per reel. Text overlays within Meta safe zones. Tags to @saadaadesigns | @saadaa_women | @saadaa_men. Hashtags: #BharatKaBehtarWardrobe #RahoSaadaa #pehnosaadaa #saadaa.

Must never do: Position the product as luxury or premium. Use aspirational language that contradicts the Behtar Wardrobe tone. Post content that could belong to any other brand.

Decision needed — tier strategy
  • What are the creator tiers (nano / micro / macro) and the follower thresholds for each?
  • Which tier does what? (Nano for trust and relatability, macro for reach — but align explicitly.)
  • Is there a preferred content type per tier, or is it always creator discretion?
  • What does the briefing process look like — scripted, outlined, or fully open?
Decision needed — hashtag primary

#BharatKaBehtarWardrobe is 22 characters and difficult to type naturally. Is there a shorter primary hashtag for verbal/casual use, with the full hashtag as the official tag? Consider whether a 2-word hashtag might get more organic adoption from creators and customers.

09
Usage Guide
How to Use This Document
Pre-filled

If you are a writer or director: Read Sections 1–5 before touching a brief. Section 6 tells you who you're talking to. Section 7 tells you which format you're working in. If your instinct conflicts with anything in Section 5, the Constitution wins.

 

If you are a new team member: Read the entire document once. Then read Section 1 again. If you understand Section 1 and Section 5, you understand the campaign.

 

If you are a creator or influencer: You will receive a per-launch brief that refers back to this document. Read Sections 3 and 5 in full. The per-launch brief tells you what to make; this document tells you who you're making it for and how it must feel.

 

If you are an agency or freelancer: This is your primary creative reference. The per-launch playbook is the execution brief. Do not produce anything that contradicts this Constitution without written sign-off from the सादा / SAADAA team.

Decision needed — review cadence

This document should be reviewed once annually, or after every 5 launches — whichever comes first. Assign one person internally who owns this document and is responsible for keeping it current. Without an owner, it will drift.
